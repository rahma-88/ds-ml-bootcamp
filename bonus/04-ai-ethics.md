# Bonus – AI Ethics & Responsible AI

> The portfolio guide covered how to present your work. Before you start deploying models that affect real people, this guide covers the responsibilities that come with that.

---

Every model you built in this bootcamp makes decisions that could affect a real person — approving or denying a loan, for example. As AI gets used more widely, understanding its risks is no longer optional, it is expected of anyone working in this field.

---

## Bias

A model can only learn what is in its training data. If that data reflects historical bias — for example, past loan approvals that unfairly favored one group — the model will learn and repeat that same bias, often without anyone intending it to.

---

## Fairness

Fairness means checking that a model's errors and outcomes are not concentrated unfairly against a particular group. Two models can have the same overall accuracy while treating different groups very differently — overall accuracy alone does not guarantee a model is fair.

---

## Privacy

Training data often contains sensitive personal information. Responsible practice means only collecting the data you actually need, storing it securely, and never exposing personal details through a model's predictions or outputs.

---

## Responsible AI

This is the umbrella term for building AI systems deliberately, with fairness, privacy, transparency, and safety considered from the start of a project instead of patched in afterward.

---

## Hallucinations

A risk specific to Generative AI and LLMs (covered in an earlier bonus guide): the model confidently generates information that sounds correct but is factually wrong. This is why RAG and human review matter for any product that shares AI-generated answers with real users.

---

## Copyright

Generative AI models are trained on huge amounts of existing text, images, and code — much of it created by other people. Using AI-generated output commercially raises real, still-unsettled legal questions about who owns that output and whether the training data itself was used fairly.

---

## Security

ML systems introduce new attack surfaces beyond normal software: someone might try to trick a model with deliberately crafted inputs, extract private training data out of it, or manipulate the data it is trained on. Treating a model as untrusted, verified input rather than a black box you can fully rely on is a security mindset every ML practitioner should have.
