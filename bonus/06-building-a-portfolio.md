# Bonus – Building a Portfolio

> The Career Path guide helped you decide which role to aim for. This guide covers something just as important that most courses skip: actually showing your work to the people hiring for that role.

---

Finishing this bootcamp means you have real projects — a house price predictor, a loan approval classifier, a customer segmentation model. None of that matters to an employer if it is only sitting on your laptop. This guide covers how to make it visible.

---

## GitHub Portfolio

Your GitHub profile is the closest thing you have to a resume that employers actually check.

- Push every project from this bootcamp to its own repository, not just your local `submissions/` folder.
- Use clear commit messages — they show how you think, not just what you built.
- Pin your 3–4 strongest repositories to your GitHub profile so they are the first thing a visitor sees.

---

## Writing a Good README

A project without a README is a project nobody understands. Every repository should explain:

- **What the project does:** One or two sentences, not a wall of text.
- **The problem it solves:** Why does this project exist?
- **How to run it:** Exact commands, like the ones in this repo's own CLAUDE.md.
- **Results:** Your model's actual metrics (RMSE, accuracy, Silhouette Score) — numbers make a project credible.

---

## Kaggle Profile

Kaggle is where data scientists practice on real datasets and real competitions.

- Enter a beginner-friendly competition (Titanic and House Prices are the classic starting points).
- Publish a notebook explaining your approach — clear write-ups get noticed even if you don't win.
- A Kaggle profile with a few solid notebooks is strong proof you can work with data outside a classroom.

---

## LinkedIn

- Add "Data Science" or "Machine Learning" to your headline once you have projects to back it up.
- Post short updates about what you built and what you learned — this is how recruiters and other students find you.
- List this bootcamp and your capstone project explicitly in your experience or projects section.

---

## Networking

Your network is your net worth. Most people get hired through a referral or a warm introduction, not a cold application into a job board — a strong GitHub and resume just make you worth introducing.

- Connect with fellow students from this bootcamp, past cohorts, and instructors — they are your closest, easiest network, and many will end up hiring or referring each other.
- Join a data/ML community (a Discord, a local meetup, a Kaggle forum) and actually participate — answer questions, share your projects, comment on others' work.
- Reach out to people already working in the role you want and ask for a short, genuine conversation about their path — most people are happy to help someone early in their career who did the homework first.
- Share your projects publicly as you build them, not just when they're finished — "building in public" gets you noticed long before you ever apply anywhere.

---

## Resume

- List projects with specifics: "Built a loan approval classifier achieving X% accuracy on Y features" beats "Learned machine learning."
- Link directly to your GitHub and Kaggle profiles at the top of the resume.
- Keep it to one page — clarity beats length, especially early in a career.

---

## Personal Website

Not required, but a strong differentiator. A simple one-page site listing your projects, a short bio, and links to GitHub/LinkedIn/Kaggle takes an afternoon to build and makes you memorable.

---

## How to Showcase Projects

When you talk about a project — in an interview, on LinkedIn, or in a README — always cover the same three things: **the problem**, **your approach**, and **the result**. That structure alone makes any project sound professional.
