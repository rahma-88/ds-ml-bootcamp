I implemented a customer segmentation pipeline on a wholesale dataset. The process involved extracting six spending columns (Fresh, Milk, Grocery, Frozen, Detergents_Paper, Delicassen), handling outliers using IQR capping (k=1.5), and scaling features with StandardScaler. Finally, I trained and compared K-Means (k=5) against Gaussian Mixture Models (GMM).

Understanding K-Means: K-Means groups data by minimizing the variance within clusters using an assign-and-update loop:|||Initialize: Places k random centroids in the data space.|Assign: Maps each data point to its nearest centroid using Euclidean distance.||Update: Recalculated the centroids by taking the mathematical mean of all assigned points.||Converge: Repeats until the centroids stop shifting.
Second Algorithm: Chosen & Why: I selected GMM because it allows for soft clustering (probabilistic assignments) and handles elliptical cluster shapes better than K-Means.||Summary: GMM assumes data comes from a mixture of Gaussian distributions.||Advantage: Provides exact probabilities of cluster membership.||Limitation: Sensitive to initialization and computationally heavy.||Comparison: K-Means achieved a much higher Silhouette Score than GMM's score of 0.0811, indicating K-Means created tighter and better-separated clusters.
Findings: I highly recommend K-Means for this wholesale segmentation task. It adapted perfectly to the data after scaling and outlier handling, showing clear operational segments (e.g., separating restaurants from retail stores). K-Means is computationally faster, easier to interpret, and provides much more stable cluster boundaries for marketing decisions than GMM.
