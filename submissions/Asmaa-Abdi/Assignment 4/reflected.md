implement: I trained a Linear Regression and a Random Forest Regressor (with 100 estimators) using clean_car_dataset.csv. I set Price as the target and dropped Price and LogPrice from the features to avoid data leakage, splitting the data into an 80/20 train-test ratio.
​Comparison of Models: Predictions difference: In the sanity check, Linear Regression provided a rigid baseline estimate, while Random Forest gave a much closer and more accurate prediction to the actual price.More realistic model: Random Forest, because car pricing factors have complex, non-linear relationships that a straight-line model cannot capture.
Understanding Random Forest: A Random Forest is an ensemble method that combines multiple independent Decision Trees. It trains each tree on different subsets of the data, and averages all of their individual predictions to produce a single, more accurate, and stable final output.
Metrics Discussion: Better metrics: Random Forest achieved a significantly higher R^2 score and much lower MAE and RMSE errors compared to Linear Regression. Strengths and Weaknesses: Linear Regression is fast and easy to understand but struggles with non-linear data. Random Forest is highly accurate and robust against outliers but is computationally heavier and harder to interpret.
 Findings: I prefer the Random Forest Regressor for predicting car prices. Real-world vehicle pricing is highly non-linear, and Random Forest's ability to handle complex feature interactions makes its predictions far more reliable and realistic for practical applications.
