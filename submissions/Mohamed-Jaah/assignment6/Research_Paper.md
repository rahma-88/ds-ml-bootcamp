# Part A — Theory: Unsupervised Learning and Clustering for Customer Segmentation

## 1. Introduction to Unsupervised Learning and Clustering

Unsupervised learning is a branch of machine learning in which a model is given data that has **no target label or "correct answer"** attached to it. Instead of learning a mapping from inputs to a known output, the algorithm is left to explore the data on its own and surface whatever structure, grouping, or pattern naturally exists among the observations. There is no teacher signal telling the model whether its output is right or wrong; success is instead judged by how coherent, separable, or useful the discovered structure turns out to be.

This is fundamentally different from **supervised learning**, which covers both regression and classification. In supervised learning, every training example comes with a known answer — a continuous number in regression (e.g., a house price) or a category in classification (e.g., "approved" vs. "rejected" for a loan application). The model's entire objective is to minimize the difference between its predictions and these known answers. Unsupervised learning removes that target column entirely: there is no "y" to predict, only a set of features ("X") that the algorithm must organize by similarity. Where supervised learning answers the question *"given this input, what is the known outcome?"*, unsupervised learning answers *"what natural groups or structures exist in this data that we didn't already know about?"*

A concrete example of **clustering** (the most common unsupervised task) is **market segmentation**: a retailer with no predefined customer categories can cluster shoppers by purchase history and discover, after the fact, that its customers naturally fall into groups such as bulk grocery buyers, occasional convenience shoppers, and high-frequency premium buyers — categories that were never explicitly labeled in the raw data. A contrasting example of **supervised learning** is email spam detection, where a model is trained on thousands of emails that have already been manually labeled "spam" or "not spam," and it learns to reproduce that labeling on new, unseen emails.

## 2. Clustering Algorithms

Three clustering algorithms are commonly used as a starting point for unsupervised segmentation: K-Means, Hierarchical Clustering, and DBSCAN. Each makes different assumptions about the shape of the data and trades off simplicity, flexibility, and robustness to noise differently.

**K-Means** works by choosing a fixed number of clusters, *k*, and then iteratively placing *k* centroids in the feature space, assigning every data point to its nearest centroid, and moving each centroid to the mean position of the points assigned to it. This assign-and-update cycle repeats until the centroids stop moving significantly. A typical real-world use case is segmenting retail or wholesale customers by spending behavior, as in the wholesale distributor project used in this course. K-Means's main advantages are its speed, simplicity, and ease of interpretation — it scales well to large datasets and produces clusters that are simple to describe by their centroid values. Its main limitations are that the analyst must choose *k* in advance, it assumes roughly spherical, similarly-sized clusters, and it is sensitive to outliers and to the scale of the input features.

**Hierarchical Clustering** takes a different approach: rather than fixing the number of clusters up front, it builds a tree-like structure (a dendrogram) by either repeatedly merging the two closest points or clusters (agglomerative approach) or by repeatedly splitting one large cluster into smaller ones (divisive approach). The number of final clusters is chosen afterward by "cutting" the tree at a chosen height. This method is often used in biology and genomics — for example, grouping genes or species by similarity — where the actual goal is to understand the nested relationships between groups, not just to produce one flat partition. Its main advantage is that it does not require *k* to be specified beforehand and it produces a rich, interpretable hierarchy of relationships. Its main limitation is computational cost: it scales poorly to very large datasets and, once two points have been merged, that decision cannot be undone later in the process.

**DBSCAN** (Density-Based Spatial Clustering of Applications with Noise) defines clusters as dense regions of points separated by regions of low density, rather than by distance to a centroid. It groups together points that are closely packed and marks points that lie alone in low-density regions as noise/outliers, rather than forcing them into a cluster. A common real-world use case is anomaly and fraud detection, where the "noise" points DBSCAN identifies are often the fraudulent or unusual transactions analysts actually care about. DBSCAN's main advantages are that it does not require the number of clusters to be specified, it can find arbitrarily shaped clusters (not just spherical ones), and it naturally handles outliers. Its main limitations are that it requires careful tuning of two parameters (a neighborhood radius, *eps*, and a minimum number of points, *minPts*), and it struggles when clusters have very different densities.

## 3. Clustering Metrics

Because clustering has no ground-truth labels to compare against, its quality must be judged using internal metrics that measure how compact and well-separated the resulting groups are.

The **Elbow Method** is used specifically to help choose *k* for K-Means. It works by running K-Means repeatedly for a range of *k* values and recording the Sum of Squared Errors (SSE), also called inertia — the total squared distance between each point and its assigned centroid. As *k* increases, SSE always decreases (more clusters mean tighter fits), but the rate of improvement slows down after a certain point. Plotting SSE against *k* usually produces a curve that bends like an elbow; the *k* at which the curve flattens is taken as a reasonable balance between simplicity and fit.

The **Silhouette Score** measures, for each point, how similar it is to points in its own cluster compared to points in the nearest neighboring cluster. It produces a single number between -1 and +1 for the whole clustering: values close to +1 indicate well-separated, cohesive clusters; values near 0 indicate overlapping clusters; and negative values indicate that points may have been assigned to the wrong cluster.

The **Davies–Bouldin Index (DBI)** measures the average similarity between each cluster and its most similar other cluster, where "similarity" combines how spread out each cluster is with how far apart their centers are. Unlike the Silhouette Score, lower DBI values are better, with 0 representing perfect separation.

| Metric | What It Measures | What a Good Value Looks Like |
|---|---|---|
| Elbow Method (SSE) | Compactness of clusters as *k* increases | A clear "bend" where SSE stops improving quickly |
| Silhouette Score | Cohesion within clusters vs. separation between clusters | Close to +1 |
| Davies–Bouldin Index | Average similarity between each cluster and its closest neighbor | Close to 0 (lower is better) |

## 4. Choosing k and Interpreting Segments

Choosing the number of clusters, *k*, for K-Means is rarely a purely mathematical decision — it combines statistical evidence with business judgment. In practice, the Elbow Method is used first to narrow down a reasonable range of candidate values by identifying where additional clusters stop meaningfully reducing SSE. The Silhouette Score and Davies–Bouldin Index are then used to confirm which specific *k* within that range produces the most cohesive and well-separated groups. Finally, the resulting segments are checked for business interpretability: a mathematically optimal *k* is not very useful if the resulting clusters cannot be described in a way that a marketing or operations team can act on. This is why, in the wholesale distributor project, k = 5 was chosen only after the elbow curve flattened out and the resulting clusters produced sensible, distinct spending profiles.

In that project, a cluster with **high Fresh + Milk spend** typically represents businesses such as small restaurants, cafés, or hotels that need a constant supply of perishable goods for daily food preparation — their purchasing is driven by short shelf-life products consumed quickly. By contrast, a cluster with **high Grocery + Detergents_Paper spend** more likely represents retail-type clients such as small shops or supermarkets, whose purchasing centers on shelf-stable packaged goods and cleaning/paper supplies meant for resale rather than immediate food service. Recognizing this distinction lets a distributor tailor delivery schedules, credit terms, and promotions differently for food-service clients versus retail clients, even though both groups appear in the same raw dataset.

**Channel** and **Region** are deliberately excluded from the clustering features because the goal of this exercise is to segment clients purely by *what they buy* — their spending behavior across the six product categories — not by *who they are* administratively. Channel (Horeca vs. Retail) and Region (Lisbon, Oporto, Other) are categorical business attributes rather than continuous behavioral signals, and including them would let the algorithm group clients by label rather than by genuine purchasing pattern, potentially masking more interesting behavioral segments that cut across channels and regions. Keeping them out of the feature set but retaining them in the dataset allows the analyst to instead use them afterward, as an external check, to see whether the discovered clusters happen to align with — or interestingly cut across — those known business categories.

## 5. Real-World Case Study

A relevant real-world application of clustering for customer segmentation is a 2023 study that applied K-Means clustering to segment the customers of Icon Yasika, a food-and-beverage business in Makassar, Indonesia, for the purpose of designing more targeted marketing strategies. The goal of the project was to move the company away from generic, one-size-fits-all promotions and toward data-driven campaigns aimed at specific, meaningfully different groups of customers.

The data used consisted of transaction records collected between February 2022 and June 2023, from which the researchers engineered customer-level features based on the **LRFM model** — Length (how long a customer has been active), Recency (how recently they purchased), Frequency (how often they purchase), and Monetary value (how much they spend). The clustering method applied was K-Means, with the number of clusters selected using a combination of the Elbow Method and the Davies–Bouldin Index, mirroring the same model-selection workflow covered in this course.

The key result was the identification of **five distinct customer segments**, including groups the researchers labeled as "lost customers," "core customers," and "new customers," each with a clearly different LRFM profile. These segments were then visualized through an interactive Business Intelligence dashboard (built in Google Looker Studio) so that managers could monitor segment membership in real time and adjust promotional targeting, retention campaigns, and marketing budget allocation accordingly. The study's main insight is a practical demonstration that combining a simple algorithm like K-Means with a well-chosen feature model (LRFM) and standard cluster-validation metrics (Elbow, Davies–Bouldin) can turn raw transaction logs into segments that are both statistically defensible and directly usable by a non-technical marketing team — the same end-to-end pattern this course applies to the wholesale distributor dataset.
